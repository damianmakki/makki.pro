Notes from a week offline

Seven days, no phone, a lot of paper. What I noticed about my own attention when the feed went away.

I left my phone in a drawer on a Sunday and didn't pick it up again until the following Sunday. These are notes from that week.

Day 1. I reached for my phone eleven times that I counted, and probably thirty times that I didn't. The muscle memory is real. My hand moves toward where my phone would be in my pocket before my brain has decided to check anything.

Day 2. The thing I miss most isn't Instagram or news. It's maps. I drove to two places I'd never been and had to print directions. Printing directions. I felt like a time traveler.

Day 3. I read for four hours straight. Not because I planned to — I just had nothing else to reach for when I finished a chapter. The next chapter was right there.

Day 4. I had a thought that I wanted to write down and couldn't find a notebook. Spent ten minutes looking. Found one in a junk drawer with three pages used. Couldn't remember what the thought was by the time I found it.

Day 5. My kids noticed I wasn't on my phone. My daughter asked if I was sick.

Day 6. Boredom. Real, old-fashioned boredom. I didn't hate it as much as I expected.

Day 7. I started a list of things I wanted to look up when I got my phone back. By Sunday the list had eleven things on it. I looked up two of them. The other nine, it turns out, I didn't actually need to know.

I'm not going to tell you to do this. But I will say: the week was cheaper than therapy and taught me more about my own attention than anything else I've tried. The feed is loud. Silence is information too.
