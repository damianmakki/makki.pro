On craft, at scale

Two years inside a huge design org taught me that craft isn't the enemy of scale — it's the only thing that survives it.

The conventional wisdom is that craft is a luxury. That when organizations get large enough, you trade precision for speed, detail for throughput, care for consistency. You standardize. You templatize. You ship.

I spent two years inside one of the largest design organizations in the world. The conventional wisdom is wrong.

What actually happens at scale

At scale, bad craft compounds. A slightly off color token appears in 400 places. A misaligned element pattern ships across 12 surfaces. An inconsistent interaction model confuses users in six different countries simultaneously.

The cost of a single careless decision isn't one careless outcome. It's a thousand.

What survives

The thing that survives scale — the thing that actually scales — is rigor. Not rigidity. Rigor.

Rigidity is refusing to deviate from the design system. Rigor is understanding the design system so deeply that you know exactly when and how to deviate, and you can articulate why.

The best designers I've worked with at scale aren't the ones who ship fastest. They're the ones who think longest about the decision that will be hardest to undo.

The practical version

Here's what this looks like in practice: Before making any decision that will touch more than ten surfaces, write a sentence explaining why. Not a justification — an explanation.

"We're changing this because the current pattern creates ambiguity for screen reader users" is an explanation.

"We're changing this to feel more modern" is not.

The writing exercise isn't just documentation. It's a filter. If you can't explain why in a single clear sentence, you don't understand the decision well enough to make it.

Craft at scale isn't about spending more time on each pixel. It's about knowing which pixels matter, and making sure the people who care about those pixels are in the room when the decisions get made.
