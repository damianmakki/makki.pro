A dictation pad, rebuilt three times

Why the most interesting AI product I use every day is a 200-line app that just transcribes and shuts up.

I have a dictation pad. It's a small app that lives in my menu bar, listens when I hold a key, and turns what I say into text. I use it fifty times a day — to write notes, to draft messages, to capture ideas before they evaporate.

I built it myself. Then I rebuilt it. Then I rebuilt it again.

Here's what I learned.

Version 1: The assistant

The first version did what every AI-powered product promises to do: it tried to help. You spoke, it transcribed, and then — eager to be useful — it cleaned up your words, fixed your grammar, removed your verbal tics, and returned something more polished than what you actually said.

I hated it immediately.

Not because it was wrong. Often it was right. But the gap between what I said and what it returned made me distrust the output. I couldn't tell which words were mine and which were the model's. The act of dictating became an act of co-authoring with something I hadn't consented to co-author with.

Version 2: The summarizer

The second version was more ambitious. You could dictate a rambling note and it would produce a structured summary: action items, key points, a cleaner version of your thoughts.

This one I used for about a month. The summaries were genuinely good. But I noticed a pattern: I was dictating at the app instead of into it. I was performing structured thinking rather than capturing raw thinking, because I knew it was going to summarize.

The tool was shaping my input before I gave it. That's backwards.

Version 3: The stenographer

The third version transcribes. That's all. You speak, it writes, it shuts up.

It's 200 lines of code. It doesn't clean your grammar or fix your filler words or summarize your meaning. It trusts you to know what you meant.

This is the version I've used every day for eight months.

The lesson I keep coming back to: an AI feature that does one thing is almost always better than one that tries to do several things. Not because complexity is bad, but because every additional behavior is a decision you're making on behalf of the user. The model is choosing what's important. The model is deciding what you meant.

Sometimes that's exactly what you want. For a dictation pad, it wasn't.
