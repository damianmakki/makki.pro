The bike I built from parts

A weekend project turned into three months of quiet Saturdays in the garage. Some notes, in case you want to try it.

In July I bought a steel frame off Craigslist for $60. By October I was riding a bike I'd built myself, from parts, with my own hands. The process took about three months of weekend afternoons and taught me more about patience, systems thinking, and the particular satisfaction of doing something physical than I expected.

This is what I learned.

Why build instead of buy

You can buy a better bike than you can build, for less money, in an afternoon. Anyone who tells you otherwise is lying or has expensive taste in components.

The reason to build is not efficiency. The reason to build is that you will understand the thing completely. Every cable length, every bearing adjustment, every thread on every bolt will have passed through your hands. When something goes wrong — and something always goes wrong — you will know where to look.

The frame

The frame I bought was a late 90s Bridgestone road bike, chrome-moly steel, lugged construction. It had been painted over twice and stripped once, badly. I paid $60 and spent three weekends cleaning it, touching up the bare spots, and applying a wax that the seller had insisted was the only correct wax for this frame specifically. He was probably wrong. It looks good anyway.

Components, sourced over time

I bought almost nothing new. Here's what I found and where:

  • Crankset: eBay, Sugino 75, $45. Took six weeks to arrive from Japan.
  • Derailleurs: A local bike co-op, both for $12 total.
  • Wheels: From a friend who had a spare set in his basement. He didn't remember they were there.
  • Saddle: New. The one place I spent real money. A bad saddle ruins everything.
  • Bars and stem: From the same Craigslist seller as the frame, thrown in when I showed up to buy.

The assembly

Assembly took two full Saturdays. Not because it was hard, but because I was slow and careful and made several mistakes that required undoing and redoing.

The cable routing on this frame is non-obvious. The bottom bracket shell had threads that needed chasing. The front derailleur required more adjustment than any front derailleur I've worked with before.

I consulted Park Tool's website approximately forty times.

What it rides like

Better than I expected. Not better than a modern bike — the geometry is upright, the gearing is vintage, the brakes stop you but don't inspire confidence on steep hills.

But it's mine. I know every part of it. On a recent ride I heard a creak from the headset that I identified, diagnosed, and fixed in twenty minutes, in a parking lot, with a wrench from my bag.

If you want to build a bike, start with a steel frame, buy a copy of Zinn & the Art of Road Bike Maintenance, find a bike co-op where you can borrow tools and ask questions, and be patient. It'll take longer than you expect and you'll enjoy it more than you expect.
